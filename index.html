The gap costs us elections

The votes are already registered. Nobody close to them ever asked.

Across the majority-Black precincts of eastern North Carolina, thousands of people are registered and active but stay home in most elections. They are not unregistered. They do not need paperwork. They need a neighbor they trust to make it personal -- and the other side is counting on that neighbor never showing up.

When these voters stay home, our communities lose the school board, the county commission, the sheriff, the seats that decide whether a neighborhood gets paved roads or gets ignored. Turning them out is the highest-leverage move in progressive politics, and it is the one paid campaigns are worst at.

The insight

The best organizer on a block already lives there.

The most trusted messenger in any precinct is the person who already votes in every election -- the super-voter. They are free to find, because they are sitting in the public voter file, and they carry standing that no out-of-town canvasser ever will.

Block Power finds those people, hands them a map of exactly which neighbors didn't vote, and gives them a simple way to change that. We manufacture the most effective thing in get-out-the-vote -- a trusted person, face to face -- at the scale of a whole state.

Five real conversations beats fifty doors.
How it works

One engine. Four steps. Any scale.

01

Recruit

We mail super-voters in target precincts and ask them to lead their own block. No staff in every county -- just a postcard and a yes.

02

Equip

Each captain gets a Strategy Pack: a large-format map of their precinct showing the exact neighbors who stayed home, plus a one-page play.

03

Multiply

Captains reach a handful of neighbors and help each make a voting plan. The app lets them and their friends do the same -- points, a leaderboard, no pay.

04

Measure

We compare turnout against matched control precincts and pull the voter file after the election. Every dollar traces to votes that weren't there before.

The keystone

A map turns "get out the vote" into a short walk.

A captain doesn't face an abstract duty. They see twelve dots within a five-minute walk -- real neighbors, color-coded by how long they've been missing. Reaching five of them is a good week, and the map makes that finite.

Their own home is the star. The work starts there and spreads outward, one porch at a time.

Why it wins

A fraction of the cost of paid field -- and it runs anywhere.

Paid canvassing runs dollars per door and needs staff in every county. Block Power replaces paid canvassers with volunteer super-voter captains recruited by mail, and replaces cash stipends with a points leaderboard. Mail is the only channel that reaches all 100 counties with no field office -- so the whole program can be run remotely, by the people who actually live there.

Proof · the Halifax pilot

We test before we scale.

Before taking this statewide, we ran the model in Halifax County to measure the real conversion rate -- not a projection. We mailed every super-voter in four majority-Black precincts, held a fifth as a no-mail control, and tracked the whole chain: postcard, opt-in, captain activation, neighbors reached, plans made, ballots cast.

The bigger project

Health. Wealth. Power.

Turnout is where we start, because power is what protects everything else. The same neighbor networks that win an election can lower a block's blood pressure, move a dollar, and keep a community organized long after the votes are counted.

Health

Well neighbors

The trusted relationships that move people to the polls are the same ones that move them to the clinic, the checkup, the harder conversation.

Wealth

Organized blocks keep money circulating close to home and put it behind the people and businesses that hire their own.

Power

Seats and say

When the same people turn out every cycle, the school board, the county, and the statehouse start answering the phone. That is the whole point.
